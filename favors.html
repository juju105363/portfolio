Problem

People need help with small home tasks, but paid marketplaces are expensive and overly transactional for micro-favors.

Hypothesis

If time is treated as currency, users will exchange skills for skills and access help they otherwise cannot afford.

Design goal

Make the exchange loop understandable quickly and reduce uncertainty for first-time users.

Overview

What I built, what evidence shaped it, and what decisions were intentional.

Core loop

I designed around a single loop: request help, match with a helper, complete the task, and exchange time credits. The interface repeats this loop in multiple places so users don’t have to “learn a new economy” before they can act.

Why the interface is structured this way

The UI is optimized for clarity, commitment confidence, and reducing failed exchanges.

Decision 1: Feed-first home screen

Start with concrete examples, not rules.

Onboarding clarity

Early discovery suggested people weren’t asking for “marketplace features” on day one. They needed to quickly understand what counts as a task, what help looks like, and how time credits work.

A feed makes the product self-explanatory by showing real task examples and expected inputs upfront, instead of pushing users into an abstract explanation flow.

Tradeoff: less personalization early on, but much higher comprehension for first-time users.

Decision 2: Structured request creation

Prevent mismatches before they happen.

Fewer failures

Small tasks fail when the requester is vague. I used a structured form to standardize what matters: what, where, estimated time, and constraints.

This reduces negotiation overhead and increases successful matches by aligning expectations before a helper commits.

Tradeoff: slightly more upfront effort, but fewer cancellations and less back-and-forth.

Decision 3: Commitment checkpoints

Make commitments explicit in a two-sided system.

Trust

Two-sided marketplaces break when expectations are fuzzy. I added clear checkpoints at match and acceptance: what you’re agreeing to, how long it takes, and what happens next.

This improves follow-through by reducing “surprise costs” and making the system status legible during the handoff.

Tradeoff: more steps, but fewer drop-offs caused by uncertainty.

Decision 4: Credits are always visible

Reinforce the mental model at the right moments.

Comprehension

Time credits are unfamiliar, so the UI reinforces them repeatedly: persistent balance visibility and immediate feedback when credits change.

The goal is to help users learn through action, not instruction.

Tradeoff: less screen space for content, but fewer “wait, how does this work?” moments.

Marketplace incentives shaped the UX

Requesters optimize for speed and affordability. Helpers optimize for effort certainty and fair exchange. The UI tries to balance both.

Reflection

Risks, next experiments, and what I would validate before scaling.

What could break

  • Credit inflation and supply-demand imbalance
  • Cold start for new users without initial credits
  • Trust and safety for in-person tasks

What I’d test next

  • Pilot in a dense community (campus) and measure first successful exchange rate
  • Compare match success with structured vs minimal request forms
  • Test whether users understand credits without explanation screens

Outcome

Produced a discovery-to-MVP narrative and a prototype that ties interface decisions to real marketplace constraints.
