The Problem

The Context: Influencer Gift Form is a Shopify app that creates customized forms for influencer gifting and seeding. These forms are the first touchpoint between brands and influencers‚Äîthe digital equivalent of product packaging.

But we had a problem. Brands were submitting support tickets saying:

"Can you allow us to customize it more?"

"It could be more branded"

"The forms don't look like our brand"

The Business Reality: Our PM was direct about the issue:

"I have improved the UI/UX substantially over the last year. But the truth is it's not 'beautiful.' I still get feedback now and then it could be improved, or more 'branded' so to speak. I don't know exactly what that means. End of the day it's a form right, but I guess it could be nicer."

Why This Matters: For brands investing in influencer relationships, first impressions are everything. A generic form communicates "you're just a number." A branded form communicates "we value this relationship."

The Strategic Tension

Before jumping to solutions, I needed to understand the core design challenge: finding the balance between creative freedom and quality outcomes.

The Critical Insight

Too much customization is particularly dangerous because most Shopify brands are indie/small brands without in-house designers. Giving them unlimited control doesn't empower them‚Äîit overwhelms them and leads to poor results. Our solution needed to prevent bad outcomes while enabling good ones.

üéØ Decision Point #2: Can 2 Templates Actually Work?

The typography insight gave me confidence: if 95% of brands fall into sans vs. serif, then 2 templates COULD serve most brands‚ÄîIF we build deep customization within each. The Typography Psychology Hypothesis

Brand personality is primarily communicated through typography direction. If we build TWO excellent typographic foundations with DEEP customization options, we can serve 95% of brands.

The bet: Depth over breadth.

Usability Standards: Preventing Bad Outcomes

"Customization freedom shouldn't come at the cost of usability."

Remember our PM/Engineer's original concern: "The reason I didn't allow so much customization in the beginning was for accessibility." But let's be honest about the actual use case: these are private forms shared with influencers, primarily viewed on mobile phones. We're not building a public website that needs full WCAG compliance‚Äîwe're preventing brands from making forms that are hard to read and use.

The Approach: Smart guardrails that prevent the most common usability problems without being excessive.

1. Color Contrast Warnings

When brands customize button/text colors, the system checks contrast ratios. If contrast is too low (below 4.5:1), a warning appears:

Why this matters: Influencers use phones in bright sunlight, outdoors, in cafes. Low contrast = literally can't read the text. This isn't about compliance‚Äîit's about basic usability.

The decision: We warn but don't block. Some brands might have legitimate reasons for lower contrast (watermark text, decorative elements). This educates without becoming the "design police."

üéØ Decision Point #4: Warn or Block?

I chose warnings over hard blocks because (A) respects brand autonomy, (B) educates about best practices, and (C) some edge cases exist where lower contrast is intentional. Trade-off: 5% of brands might ship with poor contrast, but we avoid becoming overly restrictive.

2. Minimum Font Sizes (Mobile Readability)

Typography sizing locked to readable minimums:

  • ‚Ä¢ Body text: 16px minimum
  • ‚Ä¢ Form labels: 14px minimum
  • ‚Ä¢ Button text: 16px minimum
  • ‚Ä¢ Welcome headings: 24px minimum

Why this matters: These forms are viewed on phones, often by people glancing at them while multitasking. Text smaller than 16px on mobile is legitimately hard to read.

3. Touch-Friendly Buttons & Inputs

Interactive elements sized for fingers, not mouse cursors:

  • ‚Ä¢ Form inputs: 44px minimum height
  • ‚Ä¢ Buttons: 44px minimum height
  • ‚Ä¢ Checkboxes: 24px minimum tap area

Why this matters: Influencers fill out forms on phones, often while walking, in cars, on the go. Tiny buttons = mis-taps = frustration = abandoned forms.

4. Mobile-First Design (Always Vertical)

Forms are designed for phone screens first:

  • ‚Ä¢ Single-column vertical layout (optimized for scrolling)
  • ‚Ä¢ On desktop: form stays narrow (~400-500px) with margins on sides
  • ‚Ä¢ Never stretches to fill wide screens
  • ‚Ä¢ Maintains phone-like aspect ratio across devices

Why this matters: 95%+ of influencers view forms on mobile. Designing for desktop first would optimize for the 5% edge case.

The Philosophy: Practical, Not Perfect

We're not building for WCAG AAA compliance or screen readers. We're preventing the most common ways brands break their own forms: unreadable text, tiny buttons, poor contrast. These guardrails work invisibly‚Äîmost brands never realize the constraints exist because they only prevent bad outcomes, not creative choices.
