Case Study

Influencer Gift Form — Branded Claim Form Redesign Balancing customization freedom with accessibility guardrails for non-designers

Merchants wanted their influencer claim form to feel on-brand — but most Shopify brands don’t have designers. I designed a guided customization system (two templates + brand tokens) that creates a trustworthy, readable creator experience without letting merchants accidentally publish inaccessible or “bad-looking” forms.

B2B SaaS Design System / Guardrails
Role: Product Designer Output: UI + Customization Model Status: Case Study

Core Insight

Customization isn’t “more is better” — it changes outcome quality, accessibility, and trust.

Merchants asked for a better-designed form — but giving unlimited styling freedom would let non-designers create unreadable, inconsistent pages (contrast, typography, hierarchy). Too little customization makes every claim form feel generic and weakens the first brand touchpoint creators experience.

The UX problem became: enable meaningful brand expression while enforcing guardrails so every creator can complete the claim quickly and confidently, especially on mobile.

Problem

The existing claim form felt generic and “too basic,” limiting brand expression. But expanding customization risked inconsistent, inaccessible forms created by non-designers.

Goal

Help merchants create an on-brand, trustworthy claim form in minutes — while keeping layout, readability, and accessibility consistently high.

How Might We

Give non-designer merchants enough flexibility to feel on-brand without allowing customizations that break usability or accessibility?

Research & Discovery

Turning “make it look better” into a usable system decision.

Customer Feedback Themes

Merchants wanted the claim page to feel like their brand — especially because creators are asked to enter shipping details. The form acts as a first official brand touchpoint in the gifting flow.

  • •“It looks too basic — can we make it feel more premium?”
  • •“I want it to look like our site, not a generic form.”
  • •“We don’t have a designer — it needs to be easy.”

Design implication

This is a builder UX problem: reduce decision fatigue and prevent “bad outcomes” while still allowing recognizable brand identity.

Reference Model: Linktree

Linktree enables brand expression primarily through themes and tokens (colors, fonts, buttons), while keeping layout consistent. That constraint keeps outcomes high quality for non-designers.

Key takeaway

“Allow customization where it matters (brand expression), lock the structure where it protects usability (layout, hierarchy, states).”

What I Designed

I designed a guided customization system for the creator-facing claim form:

Component What It Does Why It Matters
Two Templates Classic (serif) and Bold (sans) starting points Reduces decision paralysis while covering common brand “tones.”
Brand Tokens Logo, key colors, background, button styling (within template) Lets brands feel recognizable without letting structure break.
Guardrails Locked layout/spacing + consistent component and validation patterns Prevents accessibility failures and preserves mobile readability.
Creator Flow Consistency Product selection → shipping info → submit Keeps end-user experience predictable across brands and templates.

Process

The decisions that shaped the system — and the tradeoffs behind them.

Decision 1: Freeform Customization vs Guided Templates

Option A

Freeform editor

  • • Maximum flexibility
  • • High risk of low-quality outcomes for non-designers
  • • More accessibility regressions (contrast, text size, focus)
vs

Chosen

Two templates + brand tokens

  • • Strong starting point for non-designers
  • • Meaningful brand expression (without layout edits)
  • • Guardrails protect usability and accessibility
Guided customization

UX rationale: “More options” increases the chance of inaccessible, inconsistent forms. A guided system limits harmful degrees of freedom while still allowing brands to feel recognizable.

Decision: Built two archetype templates (Classic and Bold) to set typography tone and hierarchy, then layered controlled customization (logo, colors, background). Layout and core component behavior remain locked.

Decision 2: What’s Customizable vs What’s Protected

I separated customization into a brand layer (safe expression) and a structure layer (protected usability).

Customizable

  • • Logo
  • • Primary / accent color
  • • Background (color/image)
  • • Button styling (preset)
  • • Template choice (Classic/Bold)

Constrained

  • • Typography controlled by template
  • • Header composition within bounds
  • • Spacing scale via presets (not sliders)

Locked (Guardrails)

  • • Layout grid + field grouping
  • • Validation + error states
  • • Focus visibility and interaction states
  • • Mobile responsiveness

Accessibility tie-in: This split prevents the most common failures from uncontrolled customization: low contrast buttons, illegible text on backgrounds, inconsistent hierarchy, and unclear error/focus states.

Decision 3: Why “Classic” and “Bold” Archetypes

Instead of many templates, I picked two archetypes that cover the majority of Shopify brand vibes while keeping choice simple.

Classic

Serif-forward, editorial, “premium.” Works well for beauty, fashion, and lifestyle brands that want a softer tone.

  • • Calm hierarchy
  • • More whitespace
  • • Subtle accents

Bold

Sans-forward, punchy, high energy. Fits brands that use strong color and louder CTAs.

  • • Higher contrast accents
  • • Stronger CTA emphasis
  • • Clear section segmentation

Decision: Two templates are enough to feel distinct without overwhelming non-designers. Merchants start from a good baseline, then customize identity through tokens.

Reflection

What I learned, and what I’d validate next.

What I learned

Customization is a UX risk multiplier.

Giving merchants more degrees of freedom increases the chance of accessibility failures (contrast, typography, focus, error visibility). Guardrails aren’t “restriction” — they’re how you protect creators’ ability to complete the form.

Archetypes reduce cognitive load better than “more options.”

Two templates gave merchants a quick “tone-of-voice” choice without overwhelming them. It’s faster to pick a direction than to design from scratch.

The creator experience should stay predictable even when styling changes.

Consistent flow and states across templates reduces errors and supports mobile completion — the brand layer should never disrupt usability.

What I’d explore next

  • • Contrast checks for chosen brand colors (prevent unreadable CTA/text combinations)
  • • Mobile-first preview toggle so merchants can validate “below the fold” layout while editing
  • • Template expansion only after demand is proven (e.g., add a third archetype for “playful” brands)
  • • Measurement plan: time-to-brand, completion rate, error rate, and styling-related support tickets

Outcome & Impact (how I’d measure)

The goal is to ship branded claim experiences that are both distinctive and consistently usable. I would validate impact by measuring: time-to-brand, creator completion rate, submission error rate, and support volume.

Guardrail principle

Offer “meaningful brand expression” while keeping the experience accessible by default.
