Case Study

Influencer Gifting CRM From scattered orders to relationship visibility for brand marketers

Within Influencer Gift Form, brands are using our form to give multiple influencers $1000+ branded gifts, but they had no way to see their relationship: total spend, gifting frequency or content received. I designed a CRM system that consolidates this data and recommended a phased rollout to expand on the system.

B2B Saas Product Design
Role: Product Designer Output: Coded Interactive Prototype Status: In Development

Insight

We already have most of the information in our system.

Through conversations with our PM (who interfaces with customers regularly), I learned brands maintain external spreadsheets to answer questions like "How many times have we gifted this person?" and "What's our total spend?"

Our Shopify App Influencer Gift Form is used for product marketers and brands to send gifts to influencers. As brands gift through our app, our forms already have most of this information. This can fully make our app more complete and fit to the marketer's needs and utilize the information brands have on hand through our app to the fullest.

Problem

Brands currently can see individually what they sent to influencers through each form from each launch but not the full picture unless they externally record it with an excel or system.

Goal

Design a brand-influencer relationship overview tool that shows gifting history, their social media and content without overcomplicating the existing product.

How Might We

Save marketers hours of manual work while giving them strategic relationship insights?

What I Designed: The Influencer Relationship Hub

I designed and delivered a complete Influencer Relationship Hub that consolidates all gifting and content data into one strategic dashboard. Here's what I built:

Feature What It Does User Impact
Influencer Table Shows profile, socials with follower counts, total spend, gift order count. Includes sorting and filtering capabilities. See all influencer relationships at a glance and identify top partners instantly.
Post Column & Sidebar Clickable "show posts" button opens detailed view of all content from each influencer. Track ROI and content performance without leaving the platform.
Content Management Add posts via TikTok or Instagram links. View post type, platform, likes, comments, and date. Sort and filter by content type. Centralize content tracking and measure engagement across platforms.
Data Architecture Contact model with influencers as entities, plus deduplication logic to prevent duplicates. Accurate lifetime value calculations and clean, reliable data.

The Complete Package:

This integrated system eliminates manual spreadsheets by making our platform the single source of truth for influencer relationships. Marketers can now track total spend, gift history, and content performance in one place, saving hours of manual work and enabling strategic partnership decisions.

Process

The decisions that shaped the design — and the tradeoffs behind them.

Decision 1: Tables Over Cards — Designing for Comparison at Scale

Initial Approach: I started with a card-based layout because it felt modern and visually appealing. Each influencer would have their own card with profile photo, social stats, and gifting history.

The Reality Check: When I populated the prototype with realistic scale (50+ influencers from our actual brand data), three problems became obvious:

V1
Cards - 10 Influencers

✓ Looked clean

Nice visual hierarchy

Easy to scan by photo

Modern aesthetic

Problem: Didn't test at real scale

V2
Cards - 50+ Influencers

✗ Fell apart

Endless scrolling required

Impossible to compare values

2+ minutes to find specific influencer

V3
Table - 50+ Influencers

✓ Solved core issues

Fast scanning (10 seconds)

Built-in side-by-side comparison

Zero learning curve

Testing Process: Created prototypes with 50+ influencer records using actual data from our brands. The comparison problem became immediately obvious - with cards, you can't see who you've spent the most with unless you click each one individually. In a table, you see everything at once.

Learning #1: Test at Real Scale Early

My card design looked great with 5-10 sample influencers. But our actual brands manage 20-50+ relationships. I should have tested at realistic scale from day one, not after investing time in the card design.

Learning #2: Comparison is the Primary Use Case

I initially underestimated how much brands need to compare influencers. Through PM feedback, I learned this is their PRIMARY use case: "Who has the best ROI?" "Who should we gift next?" "Who have we spent the most with?"

Learning #3: Familiarity Drives Adoption

I didn't realize tables give us a zero learning curve because brands already track influencers in spreadsheets. By mirroring their existing mental model (rows = influencers, columns = attributes), we eliminated onboarding friction entirely.

The Decision: Chose tables because brand marketers use this tool for comparison tasks. Tables align values in columns, making comparison visually intuitive with minimal cognitive load. Since brands already maintain spreadsheets, this pattern is immediately familiar.

Decision 2: Manual vs Automatic Post Tracking — Working Within Technical Constraints

The Constraint: We couldn't auto-fetch influencer posts and engagement data due to API limitations and resource constraints. Constant scanning of multiple platforms (Instagram, TikTok, Reels, Stories) would be technically complex and resource-intensive.

Automatic Scraping

Ideal Scenario: Fully automated, no manual work for users

Reality: Requires building complex scrapers, constant API maintenance, and significant engineering resources

Manual URL Input

Tradeoff: Requires user action to paste links

Advantage: Feasible with current resources, matches how marketers already work (copying links), gives users control over what gets tracked

The Pivot: Instead of delaying launch for months to build scrapers, I designed a manual "Add Post" flow where brands paste URLs. The system fetches thumbnails and metadata automatically, still saving marketers time while keeping the MVP achievable.

Learning #4: Constraints Can Lead to Better Solutions

Initially frustrated that we couldn't auto-fetch posts due to API limitations. But reframing manual input as "curation" actually better matches how brands work - they don't care about EVERY influencer post, just the ones featuring their product. This constraint forced me to design for their actual workflow instead of an idealized one.

Decision 3: Sidebar vs Modal vs New Page — Maintaining Context for Comparison

Floating Modal

Blocks the table, forces repeated closing/opening when comparing multiple influencers

New Page

Loses table context entirely, adds navigation friction, breaks comparison flow

Sidebar

Keeps table visible, maintains context, allows side-by-side comparison

Why Sidebar Won: From PM's customer feedback, I learned brands constantly compare influencers side-by-side ("Let me check if Sarah posted more than Alex"). A sidebar keeps the main table visible while showing post details, preserving this comparison context.

Exploration with Intention:

I asked three "what if" questions:

  • 1. What if brands need to check 10+ influencers in one session? → Modal would require 20+ close/reopen actions
  • 2. What if they're comparing engagement rates? → Need to see multiple influencers simultaneously
  • 3. What if we add filters/sorting later? → Sidebar doesn't break those interactions

The sidebar approach minimizes context switching while maximizing comparison capability — essential for marketers evaluating influencer performance across multiple dimensions.

Reflection

What I learned, and where this could go next.

What I learned

Testing at real scale reveals hidden problems.

My initial card design looked great with 5-10 sample influencers. But when I populated it with 50+ records (actual data from our brands), it completely fell apart. This taught me to always test at realistic scale early in the design process - visual appeal at small scale doesn't predict usability at real-world scale.

Tables aren't exciting, but they solve the problem.

I started with cards because they looked more modern and visually interesting. But after learning that comparison is the primary use case, tables were the obvious choice. Sometimes the simplest solution is the right solution.

Familiarity accelerates adoption.

By using a table layout that mirrors how brands already track influencers in spreadsheets, we eliminated the need for any onboarding or training. The interface was instantly intuitive because it matched their existing mental model.

Constraints force better thinking.

Frustrated at first that we couldn't auto-fetch social posts, I reframed it: what if manual input is actually better? Brands don't need to see every post - just the ones with their product. This "limitation" became a curation feature that matches their actual workflow.

The real work often happens before the first pixel.

"Build a CRM" sounded like a UI challenge, but the real problem was data architecture: influencers didn't exist as objects in our system. Designing the contact model and deduplication logic was foundational work that made the UI straightforward.

What I'd explore next

  • • Advanced filtering by engagement rate, last gifted date, or content type
  • • Relationship tagging for notes, follow-ups, and campaign grouping
  • • Performance dashboards with aggregate metrics across all influencers
  • • Automated post detection via social media APIs when resources allow
  • • Export functionality for reports and external analysis
